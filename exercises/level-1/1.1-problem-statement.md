# Exercise 1.1: Writing a Problem Statement

## Scenario Context

You've settled into Relay and had that chat with the CEO about "smart dependencies." You also spent a few days getting oriented:

- You talked to the Customer Success team, who shared that **dependency visibility** is the #3 most-requested feature in their quarterly feedback report.
- You pulled support ticket data and found **34 tickets in the last 90 days** tagged "dependency" or "blocked by other project" -- up 40% from the previous quarter.
- You looked at product analytics and discovered that **teams managing 5+ concurrent projects** have a 2.3x higher rate of tasks marked "blocked" compared to single-project teams.
- You checked G2 and found that **Competitor X launched a basic dependency view** last month. Twelve Relay customers mentioned it in recent feedback.
- Your Q3 NPS survey (n=1,200) shows that **67% of multi-project teams** report at least one missed deadline per quarter due to invisible dependencies.

The CEO's message was vague. Your job is to turn it into something precise enough to build from.

---

## Template Exercise

**Deliverable:** A completed problem statement for the Smart Dependencies feature, using `problem-statement-template.md`.

**Template:** [specs/templates/problem-statement-template.md](../../specs/templates/problem-statement-template.md)

### Steps

1. **Open your copy** of the problem statement template at `my-work/level-1/smart-dependencies-problem-statement.md`.

2. **Write "The Problem" section.** Resist the urge to describe a solution. Focus on what's going wrong for users right now. Test yourself: does your problem statement mention any specific technology or feature? If so, rewrite it.

3. **Fill in "Who It Affects."** Use the data above to be specific about segments. Don't just say "project managers" -- which project managers? How many? What do they represent in ARR?

4. **Build your "Evidence It's a Problem" table.** You have 5 data sources listed in the scenario context above. Translate each into a row in the evidence table. For each one, note the source, the specific data point, and why it matters.

5. **Write "What Happens If We Don't Solve It."** Think about three dimensions: customer impact, business impact, and competitive risk. Use specific numbers where you can -- "we risk losing customers" is weak. "14 enterprise accounts representing $2.4M in ARR show early churn signals citing coordination overhead" is strong.

6. **Craft your "Falsifiable Hypothesis."** This is the hardest part. You're writing a testable bet: "We believe that [X] for [Y] will result in [Z]. We'll know we're right when [leading indicator]." Make sure your hypothesis is specific enough that you could look at data in 60 days and say "we were right" or "we were wrong."

7. **Commit your work:**
   ```
   git add my-work/level-1/smart-dependencies-problem-statement.md
   git commit -m "Complete problem statement for Smart Dependencies"
   ```

### Self-Check

Before you commit, review your problem statement against these questions:

- Can someone who has never heard of Smart Dependencies read your problem statement and understand the pain?
- Is every claim backed by evidence from the table?
- Could your hypothesis be proven wrong? (If not, it's not falsifiable.)
- Did you avoid solution language in the problem statement itself?

## BYOP (Bring Your Own Product)

Take the vague feature request you documented in Exercise 1.0 and write a proper problem statement for it.

1. Copy the problem statement template to `my-work/level-1/byop-problem-statement.md`
2. Fill in every section using real data from your organization
3. If you don't have data for the Evidence table, write down what data you *would* need and where you'd get it -- that's a valuable finding in itself
4. Write a falsifiable hypothesis for your real feature

**Note:** Your BYOP problem statement might feel harder than the Relay one because you may not have clean data. That's normal. The exercise of identifying what evidence you're missing is part of the skill.
