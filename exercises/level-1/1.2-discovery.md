# Exercise 1.2: Running Discovery

## Scenario Context

Your problem statement is written and it's sharp. Your engineering lead, Maya, read it and said: "Good problem framing. But I have a lot of questions about how this would actually work. Can we do a proper discovery before you start writing the spec?"

Maya's right. Before you write the spec, you need to validate your assumptions, understand the user's current workaround, and surface edge cases. You've scheduled a 90-minute discovery session with:

- **Maya** (Engineering Lead) -- knows the data model and what's technically feasible
- **David** (Data Scientist) -- built the proof-of-concept dependency detection model (83% precision, tested on 3 months of anonymized customer data)
- **Priya** (Senior CSM) -- talks to multi-project customers daily, knows their pain in detail
- **You** (PM) -- facilitating and capturing everything

Here's what each person shared before the session:

**Maya:** "Our task data model stores titles, descriptions, assignees, project membership, timestamps, and activity logs. We don't have explicit dependency fields today. Any solution needs to work without a schema migration -- we're frozen on that until Q2."

**David:** "The model works by analyzing task descriptions, assignee overlap, and temporal patterns. 83% precision means roughly 1 in 5 detected dependencies is a false positive. Latency is about 400ms per project pair. The biggest open question is whether users will trust AI-generated suggestions."

**Priya:** "My top-tier customers manage 8-15 projects simultaneously. They currently coordinate dependencies through Slack and shared spreadsheets. The biggest pain isn't discovering dependencies -- it's discovering them too late, after someone's already blocked. Also, several enterprise customers have strict rules about who can see what across projects."

## Template Exercise

**Deliverable:** A completed discovery document for Smart Dependencies, using `discovery-template.md`.

**Template:** [specs/templates/discovery-template.md](../../specs/templates/discovery-template.md)

### Steps

1. **Open your copy** of the discovery template at `my-work/level-1/smart-dependencies-discovery.md`.

2. **Write the Feature Context.** Summarize where you are in 2-4 sentences: what you're exploring, why, and what the problem statement told you.

3. **List your Assumptions to Validate.** Before the discovery session, you hold beliefs about this feature. Write at least 5 assumptions. For each, rate your confidence (High / Medium / Low). Examples to consider:
   - Do PMs want auto-detection, or would manual linking be good enough?
   - Is cross-project the real pain, or is within-project just as bad?
   - Will users trust AI suggestions?
   - Is 83% precision good enough?
   - Can this work with the current data model?

4. **Document User Jobs.** Based on Priya's input and your problem statement, write at least 3 Jobs-to-be-Done. Use the format: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]." Rate the pain level of each current workaround.

5. **Identify Edge Cases.** Think about what breaks the feature. Use the participants' input to identify at least 4 edge cases. The permissions issue Priya raised is a big one -- think through others. What happens with circular dependencies? Stale dependencies? Massive project counts?

6. **Capture Technical Constraints.** Use Maya's and David's input to fill in the data availability, model readiness, infrastructure, and scale sections.

7. **Map the Competitive Landscape.** You know Competitor X shipped a basic dependency view. Add at least 2 more competitors or alternatives (including the current workaround of "Slack + spreadsheets").

8. **Write Open Questions.** After processing all of this, what do you still not know? List at least 5 open questions with owners and priority.

9. **Write the Discovery Summary.** Pretend the session happened. Based on the evidence above, write your key findings, which assumptions changed, and your recommended next step.

10. **Commit your work:**
    ```
    git add my-work/level-1/smart-dependencies-discovery.md
    git commit -m "Complete discovery session for Smart Dependencies"
    ```

### Self-Check

- Do your assumptions cover both user behavior and technical feasibility?
- Are your Jobs-to-be-Done about what users need, not what the software should do?
- Did you identify the permissions edge case? (It will come back to haunt your spec if you didn't.)
- Are your open questions specific enough to be answerable?

## BYOP (Bring Your Own Product)

Run a discovery exercise on your real-world feature.

1. Copy the discovery template to `my-work/level-1/byop-discovery.md`
2. Fill in every section. If you don't know the technical constraints, mark them as open questions -- that's useful signal.
3. Pay special attention to the Assumptions table. For your real feature, you probably hold assumptions you haven't examined. Force yourself to list at least 5 and be honest about confidence levels.
4. If possible, actually talk to one colleague (engineer, designer, CS) and incorporate their perspective.
