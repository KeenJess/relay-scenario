# Exercise 1.5: Validating Your Spec

## Scenario Context

Your spec is taking shape. Before you call it "ready," you need to stress-test it. In spec-driven development, a spec isn't finished when you've written all the sections -- it's finished when the people who'll use it agree it's clear, complete, and buildable.

You've scheduled three review sessions:

**Review 1: Maya (Engineering Lead)**
Maya looks at your spec through a builder's lens. Her questions tend to be: "What happens when...?", "How do you want this to behave if...?", and "Have you considered...?"

**Review 2: David (Data Scientist)**
David cares about the model integration. He wants to know: Is the precision threshold realistic? How will you handle model updates? What happens when the model is wrong?

**Review 3: Priya (Senior CSM)**
Priya reads your spec through the customer's eyes. She asks: "Would my top accounts actually use this?", "What's the onboarding experience?", and "How do we explain this to a customer who doesn't trust AI?"

---

## Template Exercise

**Deliverable:** A validated spec with reviewer feedback incorporated, all open questions resolved or explicitly deferred, and a clear record of what changed.

**Template:** Continue working in your spec at `my-work/level-1/smart-dependencies-spec.md`.

### Steps

1. **Simulate Maya's review.** Read your spec as an engineering lead would. Look for:
   - Requirements that are ambiguous about edge case behavior
   - Missing error states (what happens when detection fails? times out? returns no results?)
   - Performance assumptions that aren't validated
   - Dependencies on other systems that aren't called out

   Write down at least 3 questions Maya would ask. Then answer them by updating the spec.

2. **Simulate David's review.** Read your spec as a data scientist would. Look for:
   - Whether the precision/recall expectations are realistic given what discovery told you
   - How the spec handles model confidence thresholds
   - Whether there's a feedback loop (do user confirmations/dismissals improve the model?)
   - What happens during model retraining or downtime

   Write down at least 2 questions David would ask. Answer them in the spec.

3. **Simulate Priya's review.** Read your spec as a CSM would. Look for:
   - Whether the feature is understandable without AI jargon
   - How enterprise customers with strict permissions will experience this
   - What the "first 5 minutes" experience looks like for a new user
   - Whether there's a way to turn the feature off (some customers will want this)

   Write down at least 2 questions Priya would ask. Answer them in the spec.

4. **Resolve or defer open questions.** Go through your Open Questions table. For each question:
   - If you can answer it now based on your review simulation, write the resolution
   - If it needs more research, mark it as "Deferred to development" with a brief note on when it must be resolved
   - If it's a real blocker, escalate it -- note who needs to make the decision and by when

5. **Update the version number** to 1.0 and change the status to "In Review."

6. **Create a changelog.** At the bottom of your spec (or in a separate file), note what changed between v0.1 and v1.0 and why. This creates accountability and helps future readers understand the spec's evolution.

7. **Commit your validated spec:**
   ```
   git add my-work/level-1/
   git commit -m "Validate spec: incorporate review feedback, resolve open questions, v1.0"
   ```

### Self-Check

- Did your simulated reviewers find real gaps, or did you softball it? Push yourself to find the uncomfortable questions.
- Are there any open questions left that would block an engineer from starting work? Those need resolution before the spec is "done."
- Does the changelog capture the *reasoning* behind changes, not just the changes themselves?
- Could someone read this spec for the first time and build from it without needing to ask you questions?

## BYOP (Bring Your Own Product)

Validate your real-world spec through actual review.

1. Send your BYOP spec to at least one person -- ideally an engineer, designer, or product peer
2. Ask them to read it and give you their top 3 questions or concerns
3. Incorporate their feedback and document what changed
4. If you can't get a reviewer, simulate reviews from 3 different perspectives (builder, customer advocate, executive) and document what each would ask

**Stretch:** Use an AI assistant to review your spec. Prompt it with: "You are a senior engineer about to build this feature. Read this spec and list every question you'd need answered before starting development." Compare the AI's questions to your human reviewer's questions.
