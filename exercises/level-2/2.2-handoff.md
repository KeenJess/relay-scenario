# Exercise 2.2: Engineering Handoff

## Scenario Context

Sprint 1 kicks off Monday. You've scheduled a 60-minute kickoff meeting with the full team: Maya, Ravi, Sophie, and David. This is the moment where your spec either proves its value or falls flat.

Before the meeting, Maya sends you a message:

> "I've read the spec twice and I'm mostly good. But I want to make sure the team doesn't just nod along in the kickoff and then discover gaps later. Can you prepare a handoff document that highlights the non-obvious stuff -- the tricky edge cases, the decisions that might feel counterintuitive, and the things that changed between your early drafts and the final version? I've seen too many kickoffs where the PM reads the spec out loud for an hour and everyone zones out."

She's telling you something important: a handoff is not a spec read-aloud. It's a translation from product intent to engineering action.

---

## Template Exercise

**Deliverable:** A handoff document that bridges the spec to the sprint plan, and a kickoff agenda that gets the team building, not just listening.

### Steps

1. **Create your handoff document** at `my-work/level-2/smart-dependencies-handoff.md`.

2. **Write the "Why This, Why Now" section.** In 3-4 sentences, explain the business case in terms engineers care about: what problem are real customers hitting, what does success look like, and why this sprint plan (not some other approach). Skip the executive framing -- engineers want to understand the user's pain and the technical opportunity.

3. **Write the "Key Decisions and Rationale" section.** List the 5 most important product decisions baked into the spec and explain *why* you made each one. These are the decisions where someone might reasonably disagree. Examples:
   - Why 80% precision threshold (not 90%? not 70%)?
   - Why cross-project only (not within-project)?
   - Why show confidence scores to users (vs. hiding the model's uncertainty)?
   - Why dismiss-and-learn (vs. just dismiss)?
   - Why no manual dependency creation in v1?

4. **Write the "Watch Out For" section.** List the 3-5 things most likely to cause confusion or rework. These are the edge cases, constraints, and gotchas that aren't obvious from reading the spec straight through. For each, explain what to do and what *not* to do.

5. **Write the "Open Questions for Sprint 1" section.** Some questions from the spec's Open Questions table will need to be resolved during Sprint 1. List them, note who owns the decision, and set a "decide by" date. The team needs to know which decisions are still live.

6. **Design a kickoff agenda** (include this in your handoff document or as a separate section):

   | Time | Activity | Goal |
   |------|----------|------|
   | 0-10 min | [e.g., Context setting: share the "why" and the sprint goal] | [e.g., Everyone understands the user problem and Sprint 1 target] |
   | 10-25 min | [e.g., Walk through Key Decisions -- invite questions and pushback] | [e.g., Uncover misunderstandings before code is written] |
   | 25-40 min | [e.g., Review Sprint 1 work items, owners, and dependencies] | [e.g., Everyone knows what they're doing and what they're waiting on] |
   | 40-55 min | [e.g., "What could go wrong?" -- team identifies risks together] | [e.g., Surface concerns early, agree on escalation triggers] |
   | 55-60 min | [e.g., Confirm async communication norms for the sprint] | [e.g., Agreement on where to raise questions, how to flag blockers] |

7. **Commit your handoff document:**
   ```
   git add my-work/level-2/smart-dependencies-handoff.md
   git commit -m "Engineering handoff document and kickoff agenda for Sprint 1"
   ```

### Self-Check

- Does your handoff add value beyond the spec, or is it just the spec repackaged?
- Would an engineer who missed the kickoff be able to read this document and get up to speed?
- Are the Key Decisions genuine decision points, or are they obvious choices? (If they're obvious, they don't need to be in the handoff.)
- Does the kickoff agenda give engineers time to ask questions and push back, or is it a monologue?

## BYOP (Bring Your Own Product)

Create a handoff document for your real-world feature.

1. Write a handoff that highlights the non-obvious decisions, edge cases, and constraints
2. Design a kickoff agenda that gives your team time to question, challenge, and align
3. If you're currently working on a feature, use this as your actual handoff -- then note what worked and what didn't

**Stretch:** After your real kickoff (or a simulated one), write 3 sentences about what surprised you. Did engineers ask questions you didn't expect? Did any of your "Key Decisions" get challenged?
