# Exercise 3.2: Tooling Strategy

## Scenario Context

The exec team approved the rollout. Lena tells you: "You've got 90 days and the authority to make it happen. First thing -- figure out the tooling. Right now every team uses different tools and formats. Carlos's team has specs in Google Docs, Kenji's team uses Notion, and Dana's team has a custom Confluence template from 2 years ago. You can't roll out a consistent practice if everyone's using different formats in different places."

Your constraints:
- **Budget:** $200/month max for new tooling (the exec team approved training time but kept the tooling budget tight)
- **Existing tools:** Relay uses GitHub for code, Linear for project tracking, Slack for communication, and Google Workspace for documents
- **Team preferences:** Carlos likes Google Docs (real-time collaboration). Kenji wants everything in the repo (Markdown in GitHub). Dana's team is used to Confluence but would switch if the alternative is clearly better. Your team (Core Platform) already uses Markdown specs in GitHub.

---

## Template Exercise

**Deliverable:** A tooling strategy document that specifies where specs live, what templates to use, how review and approval work, and how AI tooling fits in.

### Steps

1. **Create your tooling strategy** at `my-work/level-3/tooling-strategy.md`.

2. **Evaluate the options.** Consider at least 3 approaches for where specs live:
   - **Option A: All specs in GitHub (Markdown)**
     - Pros: version control, PR-based review, lives with the code, free
     - Cons: less accessible to non-technical stakeholders, no real-time collaboration
   - **Option B: All specs in Google Docs**
     - Pros: familiar, real-time collaboration, commenting, free (already have it)
     - Cons: no version control, specs drift from code, hard to enforce templates
   - **Option C: Hybrid (specs in GitHub, linked from Linear tickets)**
     - Pros: combines version control with project tracking visibility
     - Cons: more complex setup, requires discipline to maintain links
   - **Option D: Your own proposal**

   For each option, rate it on: consistency, accessibility, version control, review workflow, cost, and adoption friction.

3. **Make your recommendation.** Pick one approach and justify it. Explain why it's the best fit given the constraints (budget, existing tools, team preferences). Address how you'd handle each team's objections.

4. **Define the template strategy.** Will all 4 teams use the same templates? Or will you allow team-specific adaptations? Consider:
   - A core template that every team uses (the spec-template.md from this course)
   - Optional supplementary templates based on team needs (Enterprise might need a heavier discovery template; Mobile might need a lighter-weight format)
   - A process for evolving templates based on team feedback

5. **Design the review and approval workflow.** Specify:
   - Who reviews specs? (PM peer? Tech lead? Both?)
   - What's the review SLA? (48 hours for first-pass review?)
   - What's the approval gate? (Who signs off before development starts?)
   - How are spec changes handled mid-cycle? (Amendments? Inline updates?)

6. **Address AI-assisted tooling.** The course teaches AI-assisted discovery and spec writing. Define:
   - Which AI tools are recommended (not required) for discovery and drafting?
   - What guardrails apply? (e.g., AI generates first draft, human reviews and edits)
   - How do teams share effective prompts and patterns?

7. **Create a migration plan.** For each team, describe how they transition from their current tooling to the new approach:
   - What do they need to do in Week 1?
   - What support do they need?
   - What's the "done" state?

8. **Commit your tooling strategy:**
   ```
   git add my-work/level-3/tooling-strategy.md
   git commit -m "Tooling strategy: where specs live, review workflow, AI tooling"
   ```

### Self-Check

- Is your recommendation realistic given the $200/month budget?
- Did you address each team's current tool preference and explain why they should change (or don't need to)?
- Is the review workflow simple enough that teams will actually follow it?
- Does the migration plan account for the fact that teams are still shipping features during the transition?

## BYOP (Bring Your Own Product)

Design a tooling strategy for your real organization.

1. Audit what tools your team currently uses for specs, PRDs, or feature documentation
2. Evaluate 2-3 options for improvement
3. Write a recommendation with migration plan
4. Be realistic about what your org would actually adopt
