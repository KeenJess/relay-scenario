# Exercise 3.3: Team Adoption

## Scenario Context

You're in Week 2 of the rollout. Each team is at a different stage of readiness, and you're discovering that the hardest part isn't the templates or tooling -- it's the people.

**Core Platform (your team):** Already using specs. They're your proof point. But they're getting territorial about "their" spec format and pushing back on changes from the standardization effort.

**Integrations (Carlos):** Carlos attended the kickoff and was politely skeptical. He told you privately: "Look, I've seen process initiatives before. We spend 3 weeks learning a new format, leadership moves on, and we're back to normal. My team ships fast because we keep overhead low. Convince me this doesn't slow us down." His team had their first spec-writing session yesterday and the results were... rough. The spec Carlos wrote is more of a feature brief than a spec -- light on requirements, no constraints, no success criteria.

**Mobile (Kenji):** Kenji is enthusiastic but worried about fit. "Our sprints are 1 week, not 2. We ship small, fast increments. A full spec for every feature is overkill." He has a point -- the Mobile team's features are often UI tweaks and performance optimizations, not the kind of complex features that need full specs. But they also have a 40% redesign rate.

**Enterprise (Dana):** Dana's team already writes detailed specs, but they're waterfall-style: 15-page documents with extensive documentation that take 2-3 weeks to produce and are never updated after kickoff. Dana says: "I don't need a new template. I need my team to stop treating specs as documents and start treating them as living tools." She's right, but her team's habit is deeply ingrained.

## Template Exercise

**Deliverable:** A team-by-team adoption plan with tailored coaching strategies for each team's specific challenges.

### Steps

1. **Create your adoption plan** at `my-work/level-3/team-adoption-plan.md`.

2. **Diagnose each team's adoption challenge.** For each team, write:
   - What's their current state? (skill level, mindset, biggest barrier)
   - What's the core resistance? (is it process overhead? skepticism? habit? fit?)
   - What would "success" look like for this team specifically?

3. **Design Carlos's coaching strategy (Integrations).** He's skeptical about overhead. Your approach might include:
   - Start with a comparison: take a recent feature that required major rework and show how a spec would have caught the issue
   - Pair him with you for his first real spec (shadow, don't dictate)
   - Give him a shorter timeline than the other teams -- 2 weeks to produce a spec for his next feature, with your review
   - Define a "spec-light" option for simple features (problem statement + 3 Must Haves + success criteria -- under 1 page)

4. **Design Kenji's coaching strategy (Mobile).** He needs a lightweight variant. Your approach might include:
   - Agree that not every Mobile change needs a full spec
   - Define a threshold: features that touch core UX or take more than 2 sprints get a full spec; smaller changes get a "micro-spec" (problem + requirements + success criteria on one page)
   - Challenge him on the 40% redesign rate: "Would a micro-spec have caught the issues that led to those redesigns?"
   - Pair him with Dana for a peer coaching session (Dana knows how to write thorough specs; Kenji knows how to stay lightweight)

5. **Design Dana's coaching strategy (Enterprise).** She needs to shift from "document" to "living tool." Your approach might include:
   - Acknowledge that her team's specs are thorough -- the problem isn't the content, it's the process
   - Introduce spec amendments as a formal practice (instead of creating a new document when things change)
   - Set a challenge: her next spec should be written in 3 days, not 3 weeks. What can she cut without losing value?
   - Show her your outcome assessment template and propose that every Enterprise spec gets a 30-day review

6. **Design a peer learning structure.** Create a cross-team support system:
   - Weekly 15-minute PM stand-up for the first 8 weeks (what worked, what's hard, quick wins)
   - Peer spec reviews: each PM reviews one spec from another team every 2 weeks
   - A shared channel for spec questions, patterns, and examples

7. **Plan for the "Week 4 dip."** Adoption initiatives typically see enthusiasm in Week 1-2, resistance in Week 3-5, and either habit formation or abandonment by Week 6-8. Plan specific interventions for the dip:
   - What will you do if Carlos stops writing specs after his first one?
   - What will you do if Kenji's team writes micro-specs for everything (even complex features)?
   - What will you do if Dana's team produces beautiful specs that are still never updated?

8. **Commit your adoption plan:**
   ```
   git add my-work/level-3/team-adoption-plan.md
   git commit -m "Team adoption plan: tailored coaching for 4 teams"
   ```

### Self-Check

- Are your coaching strategies tailored to each team, or did you give everyone the same advice?
- Did you address the core resistance for each team, or just the symptoms?
- Is the peer learning structure lightweight enough that people will actually participate?
- Do your "Week 4 dip" interventions feel realistic, or are they wishful thinking?
